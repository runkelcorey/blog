abstract: 'In response to the subprime mortgage crisis, the federal government authorized the Neighborhood Stabilization Program (NSP), a relief effort aimed at stabilizing prices. Though this program resembled Democrat-supported welfare programs, there aretheoretical questions about the impacts it may have had on voting by raising prices. Political economists have predicted that increased home equity leads to preferences forless social insurance, while early 21st-century American politics consistently connectedless social insurance with lower taxation. I use a novel dataset to test how the NSP affected voting the 2010 Congressional midterms to understand how the housing policies that spurred the subprime boom (and bust) changed American politics. I find that housing operated as predicted by the labor risk--equity model, but was not sharpened by the presence of Tea Party candidates.'
